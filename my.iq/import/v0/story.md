I follow best practices ...

Linear Narrative: This is the most straightforward structure, where events occur chronologically from beginning to end without any major deviations.

Hero's Journey: Popularized by Joseph Campbell, this structure involves a hero embarking on an adventure, facing trials and challenges, and ultimately undergoing personal transformation.

Three-Act Structure: Consists of Setup, Confrontation, and Resolution. Act One establishes the characters and setting, Act Two introduces conflict and complications, and Act Three resolves the conflict and ties up loose ends.

Episodic Structure: The story is divided into separate episodes, each with its own beginning, middle, and end. This is common in TV series or serialized novels.

Frame Story: A story within a story. The outer narrative provides a framework for the inner narrative, often used to provide context or perspective.

Nonlinear Narrative: Events are presented out of chronological order, creating suspense or allowing for the exploration of multiple perspectives.

In Media Res: Latin for "in the middle of things." The story begins in the middle of the action, with exposition provided later through flashbacks or dialogue.

Circular Structure: The story ends where it began, creating a sense of closure or cyclical themes.

Parallel Plotlines: Multiple storylines that run concurrently and may intersect or influence each other.

Framed Narrative: A clear framing device that shapes the narrative, such as a character telling a story to another character.
